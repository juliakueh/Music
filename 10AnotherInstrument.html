Double Bass

String Instrument

The double bass, also known as the string bass or bass, is the biggest and deepest-pitched among all the string instruments, sounding an octave below the cello. Given its height, taller than the musician, the bassist either stands or sits on a high stool to perform, with the instrument resting on the floor (Instruments of the Orchestra – Arapahoe Philharmonic, n.d.).

Cello

String Instrument

The cello produces notes just one octave lower than the viola, despite being much larger in size. Because of its size, the cello has an end pin that rests on the ground to support the weight of the instrument. To play, the cellist must sit on a chair with the cello resting between his or her knees (Instruments of the Orchestra – Arapahoe Philharmonic, n.d.).

Viola

String Instrument

A string insrtument that looks slightly larger than violin which played in the same manner is called a viola. The viola has a warmer tone than the violin because it shares the same three lower strings, but its fourth string is tuned a fifth lower than the violin's lowest string and is often used to provide harmonies that complement the violin's melody (Instruments of the Orchestra – Arapahoe Philharmonic, n.d.).

Violin

String Instrument

The violin, the smallest and highest-pitched string instrument, is typically held beneath the chin and positioned on the player's left shoulder for proper playing posture. It can be played while standing or sitting.Typically, violinists in an ensemble sit whereas soloists stand (Instruments of the Orchestra – Arapahoe Philharmonic, n.d.).

Flute

Woodwinds Instrument

The flute is made by metal tube which lenght about two feet long, featuring a series of holes that are controlled by keys. The player can create a harsh, piercing sound or a low, soothing sound by blowing air across the mouthpiece's tiny hole (Instruments of the Orchestra – Arapahoe Philharmonic, n.d.).

Tuba

Brass Instrument

The trumpet is known for its bright and clear tone quality, achieved as air flows through about six and a half feet of coiled tubing. In the early 19th century, three valves were introduced to alter pitches in the contemporary trumpet (Instruments of the Orchestra – Arapahoe Philharmonic, n.d.).
